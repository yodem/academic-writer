### A Proposal for Integration in the University Research Environment

## The Research Reality in Humanities Today

Every humanities scholar knows this experience.

You are three months into a research project. Your desk holds 40 books, 200 photocopied pages, a hard drive with 60 PDF articles, and three notebooks filled with handwritten observations. You know exactly what argument you want to make. You can see it clearly in your mind.

But the article refuses to be written.

Not because you lack ideas — you have too many. Not because you lack sources — you have more than you can synthesize. The obstacle is the enormous labor of **translation**: turning years of accumulated knowledge, marginal notes, and intellectual intuition into a formally structured, precisely cited, academically rigorous document.

This process — for most humanities scholars — takes months. It involves dozens of tabs open simultaneously, constant cross-referencing between sources, repeated checking of citations, managing footnotes, and the cognitive exhaustion of holding a complex argument in your head while simultaneously formatting it for publication.

**This is the problem we solve.**

## Before We Begin: What Is AI in This Context?

When people hear "AI," they often think of chatbots that give shallow answers, hallucinate facts, or write generic text that sounds nothing like a scholar.

**This is a different kind of tool entirely.**

The AI we are describing — Claude, developed by Anthropic — is not a search engine, not a spell-checker, and not a text generator that invents information. Think of it instead as a **highly capable research collaborator** who:

- Has read your sources (because you gave them to it)
- Writes only what it can verify from those sources
- Learns your personal writing style from your past articles
- Follows your discipline's citation conventions
- Works in your language — including Hebrew, right-to-left, with correct academic register
- Produces output in the exact Word document format required by journals

The key distinction: **this AI does not replace your judgment — it executes it.**

You decide the argument. You approve the thesis. You shape the outline. The AI handles the labor of drafting, cross-referencing, verifying citations, and formatting.

Claude Code supports **plugins** — specialized tools built on top of the AI that are designed for a specific domain or profession.

A plugin is like a professional extension. In the same way that a word processor has a general-purpose spell checker (built-in) but a scholar might install a specialized academic citation manager (a plugin), Claude Code has general AI capabilities built in, but domain-specific plugins that make it dramatically more powerful for specific use cases.

**The Academic Writer plugin** was built specifically for humanities researchers. It does not add vague "AI writing help." It adds a complete, structured pipeline for producing peer-review-quality academic articles from your existing research library.

## The Cowork Model: Human Decisions, AI Execution

The most important concept to understand about this tool is **how collaboration works**.

The plugin uses what we call the **Cowork model**:

> **You make every intellectual decision. The AI executes every labor-intensive task.**

Here is how an article is actually written using the plugin:

**Phase 1 — Conversational (you and the AI talk)**

1. You name a research topic
2. The AI searches your Candlekeep library and presents what sources you have
3. You select which sources are relevant to this article
4. The AI reads all selected sources in parallel and maps the arguments, counterarguments, and key scholars
5. Based on its reading, the AI proposes 2–3 possible thesis statements
6. **You choose the thesis** (or modify it in your own words)
7. The AI drafts a section-by-section outline
8. **You approve the outline** (or rearrange, add, cut sections)

**Phase 2 — Autonomous (AI works, you review)**

9. The AI writes each section, one by one
10. Every paragraph passes through 8 quality checks (explained below)
11. The AI produces a complete article with abstract
12. A self-review scores the article before you see it
13. The final formatted .docx is delivered to you

This entire process — which normally takes weeks of solitary writing — takes **hours**.

## The 8-Check Quality Pipeline

This is what separates Academic Writer from any generic AI writing tool.

Every single paragraph written by the AI passes through **eight sequential quality checks** before it reaches you:

### 1. Source Search (Mandatory)
Before writing a single sentence, the AI must first query your research library and find relevant passages. **No paragraph may be written without a search.** This prevents the AI from inventing content.

### 2. Style Fingerprint Compliance
The AI checks the paragraph against your personal writing style — learned from your past published articles. Sentence length, argumentative rhythm, vocabulary level, use of qualifiers — all matched to your voice.

### 3. Grammar and Language Check
In Hebrew mode: spelling, vowelization conventions, punctuation according to the Academy of the Hebrew Language standards. In English: standard academic grammar.

### 4. Academic Register and Linking Words
Vocabulary is upgraded to appropriate academic register. Transitions are drawn from a curated list of Hebrew academic linking words to ensure the prose sounds like scholarship, not conversation.

### 5. Language Purity
Foreign terms are identified and either transliterated or moved to footnotes. No language mixing in the body text — the article reads as pure Hebrew (or English) scholarship.

### 6. Anti-AI Detection
The AI checks its own output against a list of patterns that make academic writing sound "generated" — formulaic openers, inflated language, hollow transitions, predictable sentence structures. If detected, the paragraph is rewritten.

### 7. Repetition Check
The entire article-in-progress is scanned for repeated arguments, repeated phrases, and redundant sentences. Each paragraph must add new content.

### 8. Citation Audit (Hard Gate)
Every citation in the paragraph is verified against the actual sources. If a quote cannot be found in the library with a confirmed page number, it is **removed** — not modified, not generalized. Removed. The article never reaches you with an unverified citation.

## Style Learning: The AI Learns *Your* Voice

One of the most important capabilities of Academic Writer is its ability to learn and replicate your personal academic voice.

**How it works:**

You place 5–10 of your published articles in a designated folder. The AI's Style Miner agent reads them and extracts a 25-dimension fingerprint of your writing:

- Average sentence length and variation
- Paragraph structure (claim → evidence → interpretation → transition)
- Frequency and type of hedging language ("it appears," "one might argue")
- Use of rhetorical questions
- Citation density per paragraph
- Preferred connective vocabulary
- Opening and closing conventions
- Attitude toward counter-arguments

Every article written by the plugin is continuously scored against this fingerprint. The result is not "AI writing that sounds professional." It is **writing that sounds like you.**

This matters because peer reviewers, editors, and colleagues will recognize your scholarly voice — because the AI has learned to reproduce it faithfully.

## The Six Specialized Agents

Behind the scenes, Academic Writer operates through six specialized AI agents. Each has a specific role and specific expertise.

**1. Deep Reader**
When you select sources for an article, the Deep Reader reads all of them simultaneously — a task that would take a human researcher days. It maps key arguments, counterarguments, tensions between scholars, and gaps in the literature. Its report becomes the foundation for the thesis proposal.

**2. Architect**
Based on the Deep Reader's findings, the Architect proposes 2–3 thesis options. Each option includes a rationale, a list of the key sources that support it, and an honest risk assessment (e.g., "this thesis is bold but requires stronger evidence for X"). The Architect also drafts the section-by-section outline once you choose a thesis.

**3. Section Writer**
The engine of Phase 2. The Section Writer takes one outline section at a time and runs it through the complete 8-check pipeline. It never writes more than it can verify. It never proceeds without searching the library first.

**4. Auditor**
An independent verification agent whose only job is to check citations. After the Section Writer produces a paragraph, the Auditor independently searches the library to confirm that every cited source says what the paragraph claims it says, on the page it claims. It has no tolerance for approximation.

**5. Synthesizer**
After all sections are written, the Synthesizer reads the complete article and performs a final coherence pass: Are the arguments consistent? Does the conclusion follow from the evidence? Are there contradictions between sections? Is the tone uniform? It makes targeted surgical revisions.

**6. Style Miner**
A background agent that runs when you use the `/learn` command. It reads your new past articles, extracts style patterns, and updates your fingerprint — so your AI collaborator improves as your own writing evolves.

## Why This Matters Specifically for Humanities

Humanities scholarship has unique characteristics that make AI assistance both harder and more valuable than in other fields.

**The challenge:**

In STEM fields, verification is often computational — a formula is right or wrong, an experiment replicates or it does not. In humanities, verification is textual: a claim must be traced to a precise formulation in a specific source. This makes citation accuracy far more labor-intensive in humanities than in sciences.

Furthermore, humanities scholars typically work in languages with complex conventions. Hebrew scholarship in particular demands:
- Right-to-left text management
- Specific citation formats for rabbinic and medieval sources
- Academic register distinct from modern journalistic Hebrew
- Precise use of linking vocabulary from classical and modern registers

**The opportunity:**

These are exactly the tasks that AI, when properly designed, can handle far better than any previous tool. A properly built AI can:
- Search across hundreds of pages of Hebrew text simultaneously
- Apply citation conventions consistently
- Maintain academic register throughout a 20-page article
- Learn the specific norms of Israeli humanities journals

This is why Academic Writer was built specifically for humanities scholars — not as a general writing tool, but as a domain-specific research partner.

## Academic Integrity: Addressing the Concerns Directly

Any serious discussion of AI in the university must address academic integrity. Let us do so directly.

**Concern 1: "The AI writes the article — is it really the scholar's work?"**

The scholar makes every intellectual decision: the research question, the sources consulted, the thesis chosen, the argument's structure, the sections approved. The AI executes the writing of verified claims from the scholar's sources in the scholar's voice. This is structurally similar to using a highly competent research assistant who writes first drafts under your supervision — a standard academic practice.

**Concern 2: "Will the AI hallucinate citations?"**

The Auditor agent exists precisely to prevent this. No citation reaches the final document without independent verification against the actual source text. If a citation cannot be confirmed, it is removed. The system is architecturally designed to prevent hallucination.

**Concern 3: "Will all articles start to sound the same?"**

No — because the style fingerprint is personal to each researcher. The AI learns your voice, not a generic academic voice. Two scholars using the plugin will produce articles that sound as different as they always have.

**Concern 4: "Does this undermine the development of young scholars?"**

This is the most serious concern and deserves a nuanced answer. The tool is designed for established researchers who already know how to write scholarship and simply want to accelerate the production phase. For doctoral students and junior researchers, the tool can serve as a model — showing how argument structure, citation density, and academic register work in practice. The decision about appropriate use at different career stages is one for faculty and departments to make.

## A Concrete Example: One Article, Start to Finish

Let us trace the full workflow of a real article using Academic Writer.

**The Scholar:** A historian of early modern Jewish thought, writing in Hebrew for an Israeli peer-reviewed journal.

**The Topic:** "The Concept of Divine Providence in the Correspondence of Rabbi Moses Almosnino"

**The Library:** 23 sources in Candlekeep — primary correspondence, secondary literature on Almosnino, comparative material on other 16th-century thinkers.

---

**9:00 AM — Scholar opens Claude Code and types:**
`/academic-writer:write`

**9:02 AM — After stating the topic**, the AI searches the library and identifies the 23 relevant sources, noting which are primary and which are secondary. Scholar selects 18 as relevant to this article.

**9:15 AM — Deep Reader finishes** its parallel reading of all 18 sources. It maps three central tensions in the literature: Almosnino's neoplatonic influences versus his traditional rabbinic framework; the tension between his public correspondence and his private theological position; and the gap in scholarship between his political and his theological writings.

**9:20 AM — Architect proposes three thesis options:**
1. A thesis about Almosnino's synthesis — how he reconciled the tension
2. A thesis about the gap — what the correspondence reveals that his published works conceal
3. A thesis about influence — how his position shaped later Sephardic thought in the Ottoman context

**Scholar chooses Thesis 2** and modifies the wording slightly to reflect her own perspective.

**9:35 AM — Architect proposes a 7-section outline.** Scholar rearranges sections 4 and 5, removes one section, adds a brief historiographical review at the beginning.

**9:45 AM — Scholar approves the outline. Phase 2 begins.**

**11:30 AM — Section Writer completes all sections.** Each paragraph has been searched, verified, style-checked, grammar-checked, register-checked, purity-checked, anti-AI-checked, repetition-checked, and citation-audited.

**11:35 AM — Synthesizer reviews** the complete article for coherence. Makes targeted revisions to the transition between sections 3 and 4 where the argument had a minor logical gap.

**11:40 AM — Self-review scores the article: 51/60.** The score triggers a note: "Section 6 makes a strong claim about Almosnino's influence on Yosef Karo that is supported by only one source. Consider adding a second source or moderating the claim."

**Scholar reads the article, agrees with the note**, adds a qualifying phrase in section 6.

**11:50 AM — .docx is generated:** David font, 11pt, 1.5 spacing, justified, page numbers, inline citations in the format required by the journal.

**Total time: 2 hours and 50 minutes.**
The scholar's contribution: choosing the thesis, shaping the outline, reviewing and approving the output, making one targeted revision. The scholarship: entirely hers.
